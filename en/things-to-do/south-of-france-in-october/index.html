The South of France in October

Warm light, a sea still holding summer’s heat, the grape and olive harvest, and half the crowds. A complete guide to visiting the South of France in October.

By The Editors
Spring 20268 min read Share LinkMail

October may be the best-kept secret of the Provençal calendar. The fierce heat of August is gone, the light turns long and golden, and the sea — slow to cool — still takes a swim on a fine day. The crowds have thinned, the prices have eased, and the vineyards and olive groves are at their busiest of the year.

I. The weather and the sea

Expect warm, bright days through much of the month — often 18–23 °C on the coast — cooling in the evenings, with the first proper rains usually arriving late October. The sea is at its warmest of the shoulder season early in the month; by the end it is for the brave. Pack for layers: shirtsleeves by day, a jacket after dark, and something for one wet afternoon.

“The light turns long and gold, the sea is still warm, and the crowds have gone home.”

From the notebook

II. The harvest and what’s on

October is harvest country. The grape vendange finishes early in the month and the new wine is celebrated across Provence and the Languedoc; the olive harvest begins as it ends. It is the ideal month for a wine tour, a market morning, or a long lunch on a terrace still warm enough to sit out. Most museums, gardens and sites keep summer hours into mid-month.

III. Where to go

For warmth and colour, base yourself inland in Provence — the Luberon, the Alpilles, the vineyards around the Ventoux — where the autumn light is at its best. The Côte d’Azur stays mild and far quieter than in summer; Nice, Menton and the Riviera villages are a pleasure in October. Avoid only the very end of the month if you want reliably dry weather.
