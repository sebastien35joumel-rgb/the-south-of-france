The Mediterranean Diet

Olive oil, oily fish, vegetables and pulses: the Mediterranean way of eating is one of the best-studied in the world. What the research says, and how to bring it to your own table.

By The Editors
Spring 20269 min read Share LinkMail

More than a menu, the Mediterranean diet is a way of eating that took shape around the coasts of the Midi, Greece, Italy and Spain: olive oil rather than butter, fish and pulses rather than red meat, and vegetables and fruit at nearly every meal. It is not a fashionable regimen but an everyday habit — and one of the most studied in nutritional science. Here is what the research actually shows, and how to bring it to your own table.

I. The principles of the Mediterranean diet

The pattern is easy to describe and pleasant to keep. Olive oil is the main source of fat; vegetables, fruit, wholegrains, pulses and nuts form the base of most meals; fish and seafood appear several times a week, dairy (chiefly yoghurt and cheese) in moderation, and red meat only occasionally. Meals are shared, unhurried and built around fresh, seasonal produce. It is, in essence, a way of balanced eating rather than a short-term cure — and adopting a lasting rééquilibrage alimentaire is closer to its spirit than any crash diet.

II. The key foods

Olive oil — ideally extra-virgin — is the cornerstone, valued for its monounsaturated fats and polyphenols. Oily fish such as sardines, anchovies and mackerel bring long-chain omega-3 fatty acids; the small blue fish of the Mediterranean are also, incidentally, among the poissons riches en calcium when their soft bones are eaten, as with tinned sardines. Vegetables and fruit supply fibre, vitamins and antioxidants; pulses — chickpeas, lentils, white beans — are the traditional protein of the region, filling and inexpensive. Nuts, wholegrains and herbs round out a larder that is more about abundance than restriction.

III. What the research documents

The Mediterranean diet is among the best-evidenced eating patterns in the world. The Spanish PREDIMED trial, published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2013 (and re-analysed in 2018), followed thousands of adults at cardiovascular risk and reported fewer major cardiovascular events among those assigned a Mediterranean diet supplemented with olive oil or nuts, compared with a control low-fat diet. Health authorities echo the broader picture: France’s ANSES and the WHO both recommend more vegetables, fruit, wholegrains and pulses and less red and processed meat, sugar and salt — the very shape of this diet. The EFSA, for its part, has assessed the role of nutrients such as omega-3 fatty acids in normal heart function. The consensus is a pattern associated with good cardiovascular health — not a treatment or a guarantee for any individual.

IV. Adopting it day to day

You need not live on the coast to eat this way. Cook with olive oil, keep a bowl of fruit on the table, and let vegetables take up half the plate. Put fish — including inexpensive tinned sardines and mackerel — on the menu two or three times a week, and let pulses stand in for meat several nights. Choose wholegrain bread and pasta, snack on a handful of nuts, season with herbs rather than salt, and drink water. The point is not perfection but a steady balance kept over years.

A word of caution: the Mediterranean diet is a way of eating, not a medical treatment. It supports general well-being but replaces no prescription and cures no illness. Anyone with a specific condition, a pregnancy or particular dietary needs should seek the advice of a doctor or a registered dietitian before making significant changes.

V. Questions & answers

Is the Mediterranean diet good for the heart? Large studies, notably the PREDIMED trial, associate this eating pattern with better cardiovascular health. It is a pattern linked to good outcomes at population level, not a treatment or a guarantee for any one person.

Which fish belong to the Mediterranean diet? Oily fish such as sardines, anchovies and mackerel are typical, valued for their omega-3 fatty acids; eaten with their soft bones, small tinned fish also provide calcium.

Can you lose weight with the Mediterranean diet? It is above all a balanced, lasting way of eating rather than a slimming regimen. Any change in weight depends on overall energy balance and lifestyle; for a tailored plan, consult a health professional.
