# Menton, the Gardens and the Lemon — The South of France
> The last town before Italy, warm enough to grow citrus in the open, with terraced gardens, a baroque old town and a festival built from lemons. Menton, on the Côte d’Azur.

By **The Editors** — _Menton, Alpes-Maritimes_

Spring 20268 min read Share LinkMail

Menton is the last French town before Italy, and it knows it — the houses warm to ochre and rose, the cooking edges toward Liguria, and the air is a degree or two milder than anywhere else on the coast. That softness is the town’s whole fortune: it is warm enough to grow **lemons** in the open ground, which no other corner of France can quite manage.

## I. The old town and the sea

The old town climbs in tiers to the **Basilique Saint-Michel-Archange**, a baroque church whose parvis of black-and-white pebbles looks straight out to sea. Below, the long pebble beach and the covered **market hall** keep the town in oranges, anchovies and olive oil; above, the old cemetery holds a view that is almost indecent for the dead.

“The one town in France warm enough to grow its own lemons in the open.”

## II. A town of gardens

The mild air made Menton a town of gardens, planted by the wintering English and Russians and still kept. The **Serre de la Madone**, laid out by Lawrence Johnston of Hidcote, terraces rare plants up a sheltered valley; **Val Rahmeh** is a botanical garden of the tropics; the **Jardin de la Fontana Rosa** was the writer Blasco Ibáñez’s tiled dream of Spain. Each is a small world, and the season for them is long.

## III. Cocteau, and the lemons

Menton kept **Jean Cocteau**, who decorated the seafront **Bastion** and the town hall’s wedding room; his work is the thread through the centre. And every February the **Fête du Citron** fills the gardens with giant figures built entirely from lemons and oranges — a carnival peculiar to this one warm town, and the surest sign of what grows here.

### Plan the _visit_

Getting there

The last stop on the coastal train from Nice, about 35 min; the Italian border is a walk away.

When to go

February for the Fête du Citron; spring and autumn for the gardens in their best light.

Where to stay

In the old town for the church and the market; the gardens are spread across the slopes above.
